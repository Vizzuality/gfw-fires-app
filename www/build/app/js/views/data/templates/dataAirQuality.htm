Overview

This data layer provides hourly Air Quality Index (AQI) selected cities in Indonesia, Malaysia and Singapore in Southeast Asia. Where available, AQI values indicate the risk of pollutant levels to human health. Data for all cities are expressed as AQI regardless of the nationally used standard, to enable comparison across countries and regions.

The Air Quality Index is a measure of air quality relating pollutant levels to impacts on human health, where a value of 100 is equivalent to the US Environmental Protection Agency’s standard limit for each pollutant (ground level ozone, particulate pollution, carbon monoxide, and sulfur dioxide). For more information on AQI data, please see www.airnow.gov/index.cfm?action=aqibasics.aqi

AQI values were calculated using formulas provided in the US EPA’s Guideline for Reporting of Daily Air Quality – Air Quality Index (AQI), available online at www3.epa.gov/ttn/caaa/t1/memoranda/rg701.pdf.

Data sources:

The Malaysia Hourly Air Pollutant Index (API) from the Malaysia Department of the Environment is an indicator for air quality status at any particular area. The API value is calculated based on average concentration of air pollutants including SO2, NO2, CO, O3 and PM10. The air pollutant with the highest concentration (dominant pollutant) will determine the API value. Normally, concentration of particulate matter, PM10, is the highest among other pollutants and determines the API value. Data provided at http://apims.doe.gov.my/v2/hour2_2016-01-28.html.

Singapore Hourly Pollutant Standards Index (PSI) values are provided by the Singapore National Environment Agency (NEA) and converted to Air Quality Index for standardization in readings for all reporting countries. The Singapore PSI reflects a total of six pollutants – sulphur dioxide (SO2), particulate matter (PM10) and fine particulate matter (PM2.5), nitrogen dioxide (NO2), carbon monoxide (CO) and ozone (O3). Original data provided at www.nea.gov.sg/anti-pollution-radiation-protection/air-pollution-control/psi/psi.

Indonesia particulate matter (PM10) readings provided by the Badan Meteorologi Klimatologi Dan Geofisika (BKMG) at www.bmkg.go.id/BMKG_Pusat/Kualitas_Udara/Informasi_Partikulat.bmkg.

Overview

This visualization of wind direction in near-real time combines the visualization of wind direction data from the Global Forecast System (GFS) spectral model http://nomads.ncep.noaa.gov/txt_descriptions/GFS_high_resolution_doc.shtml as well as code developed by Cameron Beccario https://github.com/cambecc/earth, to create a visualization by interpolating wind direction between measurements. The GFW model was originally developed by J. Sela (Sela 1982, 1988), and is continuously improved by the Global Climate and Weather Modeling Branch which conducts a program of research and development in support of the Environmental Modeling Center (EMC) (http://www.emc.ncep.noaa.gov/) of the National Centers for Environmental Prediction (NCEP http://www.ncep.noaa.gov/). The operational forecasting mission for global prediction for medium range (3-14 days) and for extended range (week2 – S/I) is at http://www.emc.ncep.noaa.gov/gmb/mission.html. The operational GFS consists of the final Global Data Assimilation System, the GFS forecasts (GFS), and the Ensemble forecasts (ENS). The GFS is a consolidation of the forecasts formerly known as the Aviation (AVN) and the Medium Range Forecast (MRF).

The wind direction visualization utilizes data from the NOAA National Operational Model Archive & Distribution System, which may be accessed at http://nomads.ncdc.noaa.gov/data.php?name=access#hires_weather_datasets. Wind direction data are collected by satellite 4 times per day.
